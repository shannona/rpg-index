Overview

History: 1991-1993

When "The Grand Tour" that was the heart of The Travellers' Digest came to its planned end in issue #21 (1990), publisher DGP decided it was time for something new. In particular, they decided to move beyond the scattered focus of the Digest, which had given considerable continued support to Classic Traveller, three years after it had been replaced, as well as the Traveller: 2300 game. Instead, DGP wanted to create a new magazine that was entirely directed toward GDW's newer MegaTraveller (1987) game ‚Äì which of course, DGP had designed themselves. Thus, The MegaTraveller Journal was born, with its name purposefully calling back to the original Journal of the Travellers' Aid Society,

Despite the fact that it was a new magazine, The MegaTraveller Journal was very much a continuation of Travellers' Digest, with the first issue even referred to as "The Travellers' Digest #22" at one point. However, there were also notable changes. To start with it was no longer edited by Joe Fugate Sr. & Gary Thomas: by that time, Thomas had sold his ownership in DGP and Fugate was very busy administering the company. Instead Art Director Rob Caswell, who had joined DGP in its early days, stepped up.

The goal of MegaTraveller Journal was described as an "inside-out" Grand Tour. Where The Travellers' Digest had provided the first overview ever of the Third Imperium, The MegaTraveller Journal instead was meant to provide MegaTraveller with a foundation in space that it was otherwise missing during that period of publication, something to help players and GMs avoid getting lost in the "meta-politics" of the Rebellion era. The Domain of Deneb was chosen as that foundation, both because it included Traveller's traditional home of the Spinward Marches and because it was seen as a rich area for Rebellion activity. (In actuality, most of the details of MegaTraveller Journal focused on the two sectors trailing of the Spinward Marches: the Deneb Sector and Corridor.)

Unfortunately, the MegaTraveller Journal never found its footing. The quarterly magazine published just four issues between February 1991 and March 1993. That was in part because the periodical doubled its size from 56 to 104 pages after issue #2 (1991), announcing that it was becoming an "anthology/product" rather than a magazine. Meanwhile, Caswell left after #3 (1993) to pursue a freelance career, leaving Fugate to take over for the final issue.

With that said, there was strong material in the new Journal, including the main details on the Domain of Deneb for the Rebellion era and perhaps most importantly the "Lords of Thunder" adventure in issue #4 (1993), a K'kree-related adventure written by William H. Keith Jr. for a rebooted Gateway sector. It was originally comissioned by Seeker Gaming Systems, but ultimately published by DGP instead.

The MegaTraveller Journal ended after issue #4 because DGP had decidedto get out of the Traveller business, in large part because of GDW's decision to reboot the game yet again with Traveller: The New Era (1992). DGP planned to stay in the roleplaying industry with a new game of their own, "AI", but it was never published.

In all, The MegaTraveller Journal was the fifth fully professional Traveller magazine, following Journal of the Travellers' Aid Society (1979-1985), High Passage (1981-1982), Far Traveller (1982-1983), and The Travellers' Digest (1985-1990). It would be followed by The Traveller Chronicle (1993-1997), a magazine of the New Era.

Obtaining the Magazines

Though MegaTraveller Journal #3-4 were printed in high quanities, many of those copies remained in Joe Fugate's basement following the closure of DGP. Most were later purchased by Roger Sanger, and it's unclear whether they ever reentered the distribution chain. Some final boxes were kept by Fugate until they were destroyed in May 2022. As a result of all of this, the final two issues might be rarer than the first two, despite actual printing quantities; this definitely seems true for the fourth issue, which is likely also more popular due to the William H. Keith Jr. supplement, "Lords of Thunder".

As with all of the DGP material, the MegaTraveller Journal remains lost to modern readers because the current owner, Roger Sanger, has kept it off the market. The first three issues of magazines can be found on secondary markets for low level collector's prices. The pricing of the fourth issue is less clear due to its inavailability.
